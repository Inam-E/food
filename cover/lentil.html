INGREDIENTS

  • 1 cup red lentils, rinsed and drained
  • 2 cups water
  • 2 tea spoons of olive oil
  • 1 small onion, finely chopped
  • 2 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 teaspoon of ground cumin
  • 1 teaspoon of ground coriander
  • 1 teaspoon of ground turmeric
  • 1 teaspoon of paprika
  • 1/2 teaspoon of salt
  • 1/4 tsp black pepper
  • 1 package of prepared sambusa wrappers (you can find these at many Middle Eastern or South Asian grocery stores)
  • Oil for frying1 cup teff flour

INSTRUCTIONS

  1. In a medium saucepan, bring the lentils and water to a boil over high heat. Reduce the heat to low and simmer for 20-25 minutes, or until the lentils are tender and the water has been absorbed. Remove from heat and set aside.
  2. In a large skillet, heat the olive oil over medium heat. Add the onion and sauté until soft and translucent, about 5 minutes. Add the garlic and sauté for another minute.
  3. Add the cooked lentils to the skillet with the onion and garlic. Stir in the cumin, coriander, turmeric, paprika, salt, and pepper. Cook for 5-10 minutes, or until the lentil mixture is heated through and the spices are fragrant.
  4. Preheat the oil for frying to 375°F (190°C).
  5. To assemble the sambusas, place a wrapper on a flat surface with one corner facing you. Place a spoonful of the lentil mixture in the center of the wrapper. Fold the corner closest to you over the filling, then fold the sides in towards the center, and roll the wrapper up tightly to form a triangular shape. Brush the edges of the wrapper with water or egg to seal.
  6. Fry the sambusas in the hot oil, turning occasionally, until golden brown on all sides, about 3-4 minutes. Remove from the oil and drain on paper towels.
  7. Serve the sambusas hot with your favorite dipping sauce or chutney.In a large bowl, whisk together the teff flour, all-purpose flour, and salt.

FACTS

Lentil sambusas, also spelled samosas, are a popular snack or appetizer in many parts of the world, particularly in South Asia, East Africa, and the Middle East. While it is difficult to pinpoint the exact origin of this dish, it is believed to have originated in the Middle East and then spread to other parts of the world through trade and migration.

In many Middle Eastern countries, sambusas are typically filled with spiced meat, vegetables, or cheese, while in South Asia and East Africa, they are often filled with lentils or other vegetarian ingredients. In Indian cuisine, for example, samosas are often filled with potatoes and peas, while in East Africa, they may be filled with ground beef or lamb.
Overall, lentil sambusas are a delicious and versatile dish that can be enjoyed in many different cultures and cuisines.
